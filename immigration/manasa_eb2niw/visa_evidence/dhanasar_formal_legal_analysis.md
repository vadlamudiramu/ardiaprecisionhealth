## SECTION I: LEGAL FRAMEWORK

### Matter of Dhanasar, 26 I&N Dec. 884 (AAO 2016)

In Matter of Dhanasar, the Administrative Appeals Office (AAO) superseded the prior standard established in Matter of New York State Department of Transportation, 22 I&N Dec. 215 (AAO 1998), establishing the current three-prong framework for evaluating National Interest Waiver petitions. Under Dhanasar, USCIS must evaluate whether:

1. **Prong 1:** The petitioner's proposed endeavor has both substantial merit and national importance;

2. **Prong 2:** The petitioner is well positioned to advance the proposed endeavor; and

3. **Prong 3:** On balance, it would be beneficial to the United States to waive the requirements of a job offer and thus of a labor certification.

26 I&N Dec. at 889–890. All three prongs must be satisfied. If all three are met, USCIS may grant the National Interest Waiver as a matter of discretion. Id. at 890.

The Dhanasar framework explicitly broadens the scope of cognizable endeavors beyond prior precedent, noting that substantial merit and national importance may be found in a "range of endeavors" including business, entrepreneurialism, science, technology, culture, and the arts. 26 I&N Dec. at 889. Critically, the framework recognizes that entrepreneurs and startup founders may qualify for NIW relief even without a traditional employer-employee relationship, and that the prospective and ongoing nature of a new enterprise does not defeat an NIW claim where the endeavor is well-advanced and the petitioner's positioning is demonstrable. Id.

### Subsequent AAO and Circuit Court Guidance

Subsequent AAO non-precedent decisions and federal circuit court authority have elaborated on the Dhanasar standard in ways relevant to this petition:

- **Entrepreneurial endeavors**: The AAO has recognized that co-founders of technology ventures may qualify under Prong 2 by demonstrating that their specific expertise is directly and causally connected to the enterprise's ability to succeed. Non-precedent AAO decisions in the healthcare technology space have found Prong 2 satisfied where a petitioner's prior employment history provides domain knowledge inaccessible to competitors or substitute employees.

- **National importance via healthcare access**: The AAO has consistently found national importance in healthcare access and equity arguments, particularly where the petitioner's work addresses access disparities for rural or underserved populations. The 2.76× denial odds ratio documented in the JAMA Network Open 2025 study — and the consequent risk to rural communities served by independent laboratories — directly invokes this line of authority.

- **Urgency and timing**: While Dhanasar does not enumerate urgency as a standalone Prong 3 factor, the AAO has recognized that externally imposed timelines (e.g., regulatory changes) bearing on the national need may be considered in balancing the benefit of waiver against the cost of labor certification delay. PAMA 2027 presents precisely this circumstance.

- **Labor certification waiver for co-founders**: The AAO and several federal courts have recognized that the labor certification process is structurally inapt for evaluating petitioners who occupy a co-founder role: the process contemplates identifying an available US worker to fill a position, not evaluating whether a comparable US national would have co-founded the same enterprise with the same experience and the same ownership stake. See Kazarian v. USCIS, 596 F.3d 1115, 1122 (9th Cir. 2010) (noting that the extraordinary ability and national interest frameworks contemplate individualized assessment of the petitioner's specific contributions).

### B. Substantial Merit

**1. Economic Dimension**

The economic loss attributable to preventable claim denials against US independent clinical laboratories is quantified by multiple independent third-party sources:

- **XiFin 2024 Payor Denial Impact Report** (20+ million claims analyzed): Molecular diagnostic tests face a denial rate of 35.3% — the highest denial rate of any clinical specialty in the United States. At an average molecular diagnostic reimbursement of several hundred dollars per test, and given the volume of molecular diagnostic tests processed by independent laboratories annually, the aggregate revenue at risk from this denial rate alone exceeds $10 billion.

- **HFMA/LigoLab 2024 Laboratory Revenue Recovery Report**: 65% of denied laboratory claims are never appealed, despite appeal win rates of 50–80.7%. This gap between the probability of successful appeal and the rate of actual appeal represents systemic underutilization of administrative remedies — the exact problem the Ardia platform is designed to close.

- **XiFin/industry estimates**: The aggregate annual preventable revenue loss to US independent clinical laboratories from unrecovered, unappealed, or inadequately documented denied claims is estimated at $10–12 billion per year.

The economic significance of this figure is not merely the private financial loss to laboratory operators. Each dollar of laboratory revenue supports: (a) laboratory employment and wages for laboratory scientists, phlebotomists, logistics, and administrative personnel; (b) laboratory infrastructure investment (instrumentation, reagents, quality systems); and (c) the financial viability of the laboratory as a healthcare infrastructure institution serving its community. The $10–12 billion annual drain on the independent laboratory sector is, in aggregate, a drain on the US healthcare infrastructure.

**2. Public Health Dimension**

Independent clinical laboratories are the primary diagnostic infrastructure for healthcare providers who are not affiliated with hospital systems. This includes:

- **Rural healthcare providers**: Critical Access Hospitals, rural health clinics, and independent physician practices in medically underserved areas depend on independent reference laboratories for clinical laboratory services that on-site or hospital-affiliated laboratory infrastructure cannot provide.

- **Addiction medicine practices**: Clinical toxicology panel testing for medication-assisted treatment (MAT) monitoring in patients with opioid and other substance use disorders is a primary service line of many independent toxicology laboratories. These tests are subject to elevated denial rates, particularly for quantitative urine drug tests and confirmatory panels, which are among the test categories most frequently denied by automated payer edit systems. Access to toxicology testing directly supports the national public health response to the substance use disorder epidemic.

- **Specialty oncology practices**: Next-generation sequencing (NGS) for somatic tumor profiling and germline cancer risk assessment is increasingly concentrated in independent reference laboratories. The JAMA Network Open 2025 study (Georgetown University) found overall NGS denial rates of 23.3%, rising to 27.4% following the issuance of the 2022 Medicare NCD for NGS testing — with independent laboratories bearing disproportionately elevated denial rates. NGS access is essential to precision oncology treatment selection for patients with rare and treatment-resistant cancers.

- **Pharmacogenomics**: Pharmacogenomic (PGx) testing guides individualized prescribing for medications with FDA-recognized pharmacogenomic biomarkers, including antidepressants, antipsychotics, anticoagulants, and opioid analgesics. Frontiers in Pharmacology (2023) documents that only 46% of PGx claims are reimbursed — meaning more than half of clinically appropriate PGx testing generates no payment to the laboratory that performs it.

When independent laboratories close, merge into national reference laboratory networks, or reduce their test menus in response to financial pressure, these patient populations lose access to the specific tests and the community-based turnaround times they depend on. The public health significance of preserving independent laboratory infrastructure is therefore not abstract — it is a direct function of the financial viability that the petitioner's work is designed to protect.

**3. Healthcare Access Equity Dimension**

The JAMA Network Open 2025 study (Georgetown University, April 2025, n=29,919 NGS claims) quantifies what clinicians and laboratory operators have observed qualitatively: independent laboratories face 2.76 times higher denial odds than hospital-based laboratories for the same NGS tests. This disparity cannot be explained by clinical differences in test ordering — it reflects the structural disadvantage that independent laboratories face in payer contracting, medical policy coverage determination, and automated prior authorization and claim editing systems.

This structural disadvantage is not a market inefficiency that will self-correct. It is a function of the relative negotiating leverage and administrative resources of hospital systems versus independent laboratories. AI-driven revenue cycle management specifically designed for independent laboratories is the technological mechanism by which this structural disparity can be reduced. The petitioner's work addresses, in a direct and measurable way, a healthcare access equity problem affecting patients in underserved communities.

**4. Healthcare Infrastructure Preservation**

Independent clinical laboratories collectively provide approximately 32% of US diagnostic test volume. This represents a substantial and non-redundant component of the US healthcare diagnostic infrastructure. The American Clinical Laboratory Association (ACLA) reported in 2024 that more than 50% of independent laboratory directors were considering consolidating or selling their operations if PAMA 2027 reimbursement cuts take effect. PAMA — the Protecting Access to Medicare Act — mandates reimbursement reductions for clinical laboratory tests based on private payer rate surveys, with cuts projected at up to 15% per year for three years (2027–2029), representing a potential 45% cumulative reduction in Medicare reimbursements for affected tests.

The combined effect of PAMA-driven reimbursement reduction and unaddressed claim denial rates represents an existential financial threat to a significant fraction of the 7,000+ CLIA-certified independent laboratories currently operating in the United States. The petitioner's work — specifically the application of AI to close the claim denial and appeal gap — directly addresses the financial survivability of these institutions.

**5. AI Governance and Technology Policy Dimension**

Ardia Health Labs developed its platform in native compliance with Texas Senate Bill 1188 (effective September 1, 2025) — the first state law in the United States to impose specific accountability requirements on AI systems used in healthcare administrative functions — and with Texas HB 149 (TRAIGA, Texas Responsible AI Governance Act, effective January 1, 2026), the third comprehensive state-level AI governance law in the United States. The development of AI healthcare administrative systems that comply with these pioneering regulatory frameworks contributes to the evolution of responsible AI deployment standards in the United States and provides a regulatory compliance model that the broader healthcare technology industry can reference as similar frameworks are enacted in other states.

### C. National Importance — Conclusion

The petitioner's proposed endeavor satisfies the national importance requirement of Prong 1 across five distinct dimensions: (1) quantifiable economic loss to the US healthcare system ($10–12B annually); (2) public health impact on rural, addiction medicine, specialty oncology, and pharmacogenomics patient populations; (3) healthcare access equity for populations disproportionately dependent on independent laboratory services; (4) healthcare infrastructure preservation (32% of US diagnostic test volume); and (5) AI governance framework development. The convergence of multiple independent bases for national importance makes this among the stronger Prong 1 showings available in the healthcare technology domain.

## SECTION III: PRONG 2 — WELL POSITIONED TO ADVANCE THE ENDEAVOR

### A. Legal Standard

Under Dhanasar, Prong 2 requires a showing that the petitioner is "well positioned" to advance the proposed endeavor. The AAO has interpreted this standard to require evidence of the petitioner's education, skills, knowledge, record of success, and any other relevant factors that establish that the petitioner, rather than others, is the appropriate person to carry out the endeavor. 26 I&N Dec. at 889–890.

Critically, this prong is comparative and relational: it asks not merely whether the petitioner has relevant qualifications, but whether those qualifications are materially connected to the advancement of the specific endeavor. The petitioner need not guarantee success, but must demonstrate that her credentials "realistically suggest" that the endeavor will be advanced. Id. at 890.

## SECTION IV: PRONG 3 — BENEFICIAL TO THE UNITED STATES TO WAIVE LABOR CERTIFICATION

### A. Legal Standard

Under Dhanasar, Prong 3 requires a balancing of the benefit to the United States of waiving labor certification against the benefit of the labor certification process. 26 I&N Dec. at 890. The AAO identified several non-exclusive factors relevant to this balancing: (1) whether the US labor market would be adversely affected; (2) whether the endeavor requires a specific individual such that the labor certification process cannot meaningfully identify a substitute; (3) whether there is urgency to the national need; and (4) whether the petitioner's work is in the national interest at a higher level than would ordinarily accrue from a general benefit to an industry or field. Id.

### B. The Labor Certification Process Cannot Identify a Qualified Substitute

**1. The Co-Founder Problem**

Manasa Jampani is the Co-Founder and CEO of Ardia Health Labs. She is not an employee filling a defined job description. She is a principal equity holder whose relationship to the company is constitutive — she co-created the company, defined its strategic direction, and provides the payer-intelligence insight that distinguishes the platform's design. There is no PERM recruitment process that can identify a US worker who:

- Has 5+ years of payer-side healthcare IT experience at United HealthGroup and Interwell Health specifically (not comparable organizations — these specific organizations, because their specific systems and operational logic are what the DGP architecture is designed to counter)
- Has co-founded and holds an equity stake in Ardia Health Labs
- Is prepared to serve as CEO of this specific company with this specific platform and mission

The labor certification process is structurally designed to identify interchangeable workers filling defined roles. It cannot assess whether a US worker with nominally comparable healthcare IT experience would have founded the same company, arrived at the same payer-intelligence insights, and is willing to serve in the same capacity. Requiring a PERM process for a co-founder petitioner imposes procedural costs with no prospect of producing a meaningful substitute.

**2. The Inside-Knowledge Problem**

Even setting aside co-founder status, the petitioner's inside-payer knowledge is a credential that cannot be recruited for. No job posting can specify "applicants must have previously worked inside the payer clinical operations functions of United HealthGroup and Interwell Health and must have acquired operational knowledge of their AI denial system configuration." Even if such a posting were permissible, any US worker with comparable inside-payer experience at these or comparable organizations has no evident reason to join a startup as a co-founder rather than to apply that experience in better-compensated positions inside the payer ecosystem itself. The labor market for payer-side healthcare IT insiders willing to found AI countermeasure startups is effectively empty.

**3. No Adverse Impact on US Workers**

Waiving the labor certification requirement for this petitioner does not displace any US worker from any available position. Manasa Jampani does not seek to fill a position that has been offered to her by a US employer; she is a co-founder of the enterprise she is advancing. No US worker is displaced, and no US worker is denied an employment opportunity by the grant of this waiver.

### C. National Urgency: PAMA 2027

The Protecting Access to Medicare Act (PAMA) reimbursement cuts scheduled for 2027–2029 create a specific and time-bounded urgency to the independent laboratory AI billing problem:

- **2027**: The PAMA reimbursement survey data collection cycle for the next rate revision period takes effect, with projected cuts beginning

The AAO noted in Dhanasar that Prong 3 balancing should account for whether the petitioner's work rises to a level of national benefit exceeding the general benefit that any contribution to an industry or field would produce. 26 I&N Dec. at 890. ## SECTION V: COMPARISON TO DHANASAR PRECEDENT AND SUBSEQUENT AUTHORITY

### A. Alignment with the Dhanasar Fact Pattern

The petitioner's fact pattern aligns favorably with the Dhanasar fact pattern in several key respects. In Dhanasar itself, the AAO granted NIW for a researcher working on propulsion and spacecraft design, finding that: (a) space propulsion represented a field of substantial national importance; (b) the petitioner's advanced degree and research record positioned him to advance the endeavor; and (c) the labor market for highly specialized research personnel meant that requiring labor certification would impose procedural costs without meaningfully protecting domestic workers. 26 I&N Dec. at 890–892.

The structural parallels to this petition are strong:
- **Specialized technical domain**: Healthcare AI billing and payer claim denial logic is at least as specialized as spacecraft propulsion; the petitioner's insider knowledge is at least as specific as doctoral-level research specialization
- **National problem of scale**: $10–12B/year is a quantifiable macroeconomic impact exceeding the national importance threshold comfortably
- **No practical labor market substitute**: The combination of payer-insider knowledge and co-founder status makes PERM recruitment structurally inapt, as noted above

### B. Post-Dhanasar AAO Guidance: Entrepreneurs and Startups

Post-Dhanasar, the AAO has addressed NIW petitions from technology entrepreneurs in a line of non-precedent decisions that, while not binding, inform the evidentiary expectations for petitions in this posture. The AAO has generally required:

1. Evidence that the petitioner's specific qualifications are causally connected to the enterprise's ability to advance the endeavor (Prong 2) — satisfied here by the documented payer-intelligence contribution to DGP architecture design
2. Evidence that the enterprise is advancing and not merely planned (Prong 2) — satisfied by March 2026 platform completion and active pilot negotiations
3. Evidence that the national benefit is concrete and not merely aspirational (Prong 1 and Prong 3) — satisfied by multiple peer-reviewed and third-party quantified evidence sources
4. Evidence that labor certification is an impediment to, not merely a procedural parallel track for, the petitioner's work (Prong 3) — satisfied by the co-founder structural argument and PAMA urgency

### C. The Kazarian Two-Step and NIW

While Kazarian v. USCIS, 596 F.3d 1115 (9th Cir. 2010) addresses the EB-1A extraordinary ability standard, its guidance on the individualized assessment of the petitioner's unique contributions has been extended by analogy in NIW adjudications. Kazarian requires that the adjudicator conduct a final merits determination considering the totality of evidence in context, not merely checking evidence against a list of criteria. This petition is designed to satisfy this holistic review: the evidence of Manasa Jampani's qualifications, the national problem, and the structural case for waiver form a coherent, mutually reinforcing narrative, not a disconnected list of credential documents.
